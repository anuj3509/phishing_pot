In this issue, Angela Bassa talks about going from being an individual
contributor to a manager in data science.*
[image: Angela Bassa]

BY *Angela Bassa*

BY *Angela Bassa*

Great data scientists have career options and won’t abide bad managers for
very long. If you want to retain them, you need to care about their work,
connect it to the business, and design a diverse, resilient,
high-performing team.

*Build trust and be candid*
Trust, authenticity, and loyalty are particularly essential in data
science. Why? There’s often confusion around the discipline and its role in
the company, so the team manager is responsible for insulating data
scientists from unreasonable requests and for explaining the team’s role to
the rest of the organization. The best way to build trust is to make sure
that your team members have interesting projects to work on, and that
they’re not burdened by projects with vague requirements or unrealistic
timelines.

You should also go out of your way to be candid. Data scientists are smart
people who are trained in how to interrogate and handle information. My
heuristic is to be about 20% more direct and candid than you think you
should be.

*Connect the work to the business*
To get the most from a data scientist’s time, give them a clear picture of
the business goal behind a project. A lot of managing data science is
discussing and fine-tuning questions from stakeholders so that you better
understand the information they actually want and how it will be used.
Don’t let questions or requests become projects for your team until you
know exactly what the stakeholder wants and how they’ll use it. Requiring
very clear objectives for the data questions that come your way is one of
the most important things you can do for your team. Stakeholders can’t
always ask these kinds of focused, specific questions on their own; make
sure members of the data science team are regularly invited to product and
strategy meetings.

*Design great teams*
As a data science manager, you’ll get lots of résumés. Take advantage of
that to be picky in the right ways. Social skills like empathy and
communication are undervalued in data science and should be part of your
hiring criteria. And instead of hiring just for “culture fit,” ask yourself
if this person sees the world in a way that expands the boundaries of the
team’s knowledge.

Don’t just hire senior people — less-experienced employees tend not to make
the assumptions that they do. And embrace a diversity of academic
backgrounds. I have a team member who studied marine biology, and their
knowledge of how pods of dolphins behave in the wild is surprisingly useful
when modeling a fleet of robots. Finally, build a team that reflects the
people whose data you’re analyzing — that team will ask better questions.

*When to specialize*
One final piece of advice: When a data science team is just starting out,
everyone on it will wear many hats and do lots of different kinds of data
science. That’s OK — it’s like when someone joins a startup. As your team
matures and proves its value, recognize that roles will become more defined
and some activity will move to other teams (infrastructure and ops, for
example). But specialization works only when clear, well-defined
requirements are available to offset the coordination delays and costs of
multiple teams working together.

To manage data scientists, you have to understand what data science is.
Here’s Hilary Mason, founder of Fast Forward Labs, *in an interview with
HBR in 2017*
<https://link.hbr.org/click/6733778497fd0759c75a598c/aHR0cHM6Ly9oYnIub3JnLzIwMTcvMDcvaG93LWFpLWZpdHMtaW50by15b3VyLWRhdGEtc2NpZW5jZS10ZWFtP3V0bV9tZWRpdW09ZW1haWwmdXRtX3NvdXJjZT1uZXdzbGV0dGVyX3NlcmllcyZ1dG1fY2FtcGFpZ249dXRtX2NhbXBhaWduJTNEZHNmeTIzX3QyJmRlbGl2ZXJ5TmFtZT0/672a2ed6960912aa8c0e14fdB6db05e88>
*:* *“I’d break [data science] down into three capabilities*, all of which
rely on the same technology. The first capability is understanding the
business. That’s analytics, or business intelligence — being able to ask
questions and analyze information to make better decisions. It’s usually
run out of the CFO or COO’s office. It’s not necessarily a technical
domain. The second capability is product data science: building algorithms
and systems — which may use machine learning and AI — that actually improve
the product. This is where things like spam filters, recommendation
systems, search algorithms, and data visualization come in. This capability
usually sits under a line of business and is run out of product development
or engineering. The last data capability is one that tends to get neglected
or lumped in with product data science. It’s an R&D capability — using data
to open up new product, new business, and new revenue opportunities.”

style="word-break:break-word;color:#000000;font-size:16px">Great data scientists have career options and won’t abide bad managers for very long. If you want to retain them, you need to care about their work, connect it to the business, and design a diverse, resilient, high-performing team.<br>

<br><strong>Build trust and be candid</strong><br>

Trust, authenticity, and loyalty are particularly essential in data science. Why? There’s often confusion around the discipline and its role in the company, so the team manager is responsible for insulating data scientists from unreasonable requests and for explaining the team’s role to the rest of the organization. The best way to build trust is to make sure that your team members have interesting projects to work on, and that they’re not burdened by projects with vague requirements or unrealistic timelines.

<br><br>You should also go out of your way to be candid. Data scientists are smart people who are trained in how to interrogate and handle information. My heuristic is to be about 20% more direct and candid than you think you should be.<br><br><strong>Connect the work to the business</strong><br>

To get the most from a data scientist’s time, give them a clear picture of the business goal behind a project. A lot of managing data science is discussing and fine-tuning questions from stakeholders so that you better understand the information they actually want and how it will be used. Don’t let questions or requests become projects for your team until you know exactly what the stakeholder wants and how they’ll use it. Requiring very clear objectives for the data questions that come your way is one of the most important things you can do for your team. Stakeholders can’t always ask these kinds of focused, specific questions on their own; make sure members of the data science team are regularly invited to product and strategy meetings.

<br><br><strong>Design great teams</strong><br>As a data science manager, you’ll get lots of résumés. Take advantage of that to be picky in the right ways. Social skills like empathy and communication are undervalued in data science and should be part of your hiring criteria. And instead of hiring just for “culture fit,” ask yourself if this person sees the world in a way that expands the boundaries of the team’s knowledge.<br><br>

Don’t just hire senior people — less-experienced employees tend not to make the assumptions that they do. And embrace a diversity of academic backgrounds. I have a team member who studied marine biology, and their knowledge of how pods of dolphins behave in the wild is surprisingly useful when modeling a fleet of robots. Finally, build a team that reflects the people whose data you’re analyzing — that team will ask better questions.<br><br><strong>When to specialize</strong><br>

One final piece of advice: When a data science team is just starting out, everyone on it will wear many hats and do lots of different kinds of data science. That’s OK — it’s like when someone joins a startup. As your team matures and proves its value, recognize that roles will become more defined and some activity will move to other teams (infrastructure and ops, for example). But specialization works only when clear, well-defined requirements are available to offset the coordination delays and costs of multiple teams working together.</span>

<span style="word-break:break-word;color:#000000;font-size:16px">To manage data scientists, you have to understand what data science is. Here’s Hilary Mason, founder of Fast Forward Labs, <span style="word-break:break-word;color:#5272f1"><a href="https://link.hbr.org/click/6733778497fd0759c75a598c/aHR0cHM6Ly9oYnIub3JnLzIwMTcvMDcvaG93LWFpLWZpdHMtaW50by15b3VyLWRhdGEtc2NpZW5jZS10ZWFtP3V0bV9tZWRpdW09ZW1haWwmdXRtX3NvdXJjZT1uZXdzbGV0dGVyX3NlcmllcyZ1dG1fY2FtcGFpZ249dXRtX2NhbXBhaWduJTNEZHNmeTIzX3QyJmRlbGl2ZXJ5TmFtZT0/672a2ed6960912aa8c0e14fdB6db05e88" target="_blank" style="text-decoration:none;color:#5272f1" rel="noopener"><strong><span style="word-break:break-word;color:#5272f1">in an interview with HBR in 2017</span></strong></a></span><strong>:</strong>&nbsp;<strong>“I’d break [data science] down into three capabilities</strong>, all of which rely on the same technology. It’s usually run out of the CFO or COO’s office. It’s not necessarily a technical domain.
